about

I'm visual communication designer, educator, and and ethical design researcher working across creative pedagogy, UX design, and digital ethics. My work blends visual communication with ethical inquiry, positioning design as a site of relational accountability, cultural awareness, and presence.
Grounded in decolonial design, Indigenous data sovereignty, and community-guided methodologies, my practiceis shaped through long-term collaboration with Indigenous Elders and knowledge keepers. I explore how digital systems can uphold consent, transparency, and cultural integrity by integrating human-centered design with Indigenous epistemologies.
My scholarship spans design, digital humanities, and Indigenous data sovereignty, extending into decolonial design ethics, AI governance, and critical knowledge infrastructures. I holds a Practice-Based PhD in Graphic Design and Digital Media from Anglia Ruskin University, an MDes from he University of Alberta, and a BDes from OCAD University.

Research

Teaching
As a post-secondary educator, I centre creative problem-solving, creative intelligence, and perpetual intelligence: the lifelong capacity to keep learning, adapting, and making meaning in complex contexts. My teaching practice is grounded in designing learning experiences that actively strengthen these human capacities through an empathy- and example-based learning approach.
Teaching Philosophy
My teaching is grounded in empathy-based learning. Empathy is essential to education because it allows us to understand the needs, experiences, and emotional realities of our students. When students feel seen, respected, and supported, the classroom becomes a space of safety and possibility. I have always understood teaching as parallel to design. Designers create meaning through empathy—by entering the world of the end user and shaping experiences that resonate with their lives. Teaching operates in the same way: it requires designing learning experiences that are relational, intuitive, and responsive to the learner.

To support this, I use example-based learning, a pedagogy that helps students understand complex ideas through themes, contexts, and references they already recognize. When students encounter material through examples rooted in the familiar, they grasp not only the concept but its relevance. This reinforces empathy—because it affirms students’ lived experiences as valid sites of knowledge—and it strengthens practice-based learning by connecting theory to real situations they might face as emerging designers. In my classroom, examples are not add-ons but bridges: they translate abstract ideas into something tangible, meaningful, and memorable.

Empathy-based learning also aligns with the nurturing qualities found in many Indigenous knowledge systems, particularly the idea of the “renewal of the feminine spirit,” where teaching emerges from care, reciprocity, and emotional intelligence. I work to cultivate a respectful, open environment where students’ voices matter and where dialogue becomes part of the learning process. In practice-based fields like design and digital media, I believe educators function as mentors, and students should be treated as junior practitioners. Teaching them as practitioners-in-training builds trust, accountability, and confidence. It encourages students to see themselves not simply as learners but as contributors with growing agency and responsibility.

Ultimately, I aim to build an inclusive, relational learning environment—one where empathy, example-based understanding, and practice-based exploration come together. When these elements intersect, students feel supported and motivated, and they begin to see learning as a collaborative, transformative experience rather than transactional.
